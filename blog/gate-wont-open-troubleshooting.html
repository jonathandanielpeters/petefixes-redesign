My Gate Won't Open: Troubleshooting Guide for Winnipeg Gate Owners

April 5, 2026 · 7 min read

You're pulling into your driveway, you press the remote, and nothing happens. Your automatic gate won't open. Before you panic (or start climbing the fence), work through this troubleshooting guide. Most gate problems have simple causes, and many can be fixed without a service call.

Step 1: Check the Power

The number one reason a gate opener won't work is a power issue. Start here:

  • Check the circuit breaker. Gate operators are usually on a dedicated breaker. A power surge, lightning strike, or overloaded circuit can trip it. Flip it off and back on.
  • Check for a power outage. If your street lights and neighbours' lights are out, the gate is waiting for power just like everything else. Use the manual release to open the gate (see below).
  • Check the battery. Many gate operators have a backup battery. If the battery is dead and power was interrupted, the operator may not restart automatically. Solar-powered systems can lose charge after several cloudy winter days.
  • Look for indicator lights. Most operators have LED status lights on the control board. A solid red light, flashing pattern, or no light at all each mean different things — check your operator manual for the code.

Step 2: Check the Remote and Keypad

  • Replace the remote battery. This solves more service calls than you'd think. Remotes use CR2032 or similar coin batteries that last 1-2 years.
  • Try the wall button or keypad. If the wall button works but the remote doesn't, the remote is the problem, not the gate.
  • Try a different remote. If you have a spare, test it. If neither works, the receiver in the operator may need attention.
  • Check the keypad. In Winnipeg winters, keypads can freeze, condensation can short-circuit contacts, and the display may go blank in extreme cold. Many keypad fob systems have weather covers — make sure yours is sealed.

Step 3: Check the Safety Sensors

All automatic gates have safety devices — photo eyes, sensor loops, or edge sensors — that prevent the gate from closing on people, vehicles, or objects. If a sensor is tripped, the gate won't move.

  • Photo eyes: Check that the small sensors on either side of the gate opening are aligned and not blocked by snow, ice, spider webs, or debris. Clean the lenses with a dry cloth.
  • Ground loops (vehicle detectors): These are wire loops buried under the driveway that detect vehicles. If the loop is damaged (from driveway cracks, frost heave, or repaving), the gate may think a vehicle is in the way.
  • Edge sensors: Some gates have pressure-sensitive strips along the leading edge. If the strip is damaged or frozen, the gate may reverse or refuse to close.
Manitoba Winter Tip After a heavy snowfall, check the photo eye sensors first. Snow buildup on or in front of the sensors is the most common winter gate issue in Winnipeg. A quick brush-off is all it takes.

Step 4: Check for Physical Obstructions

Slide and Cantilever Gates

  • Track or rail blockage: Snow, ice, gravel, or debris in a ground track will stop a slide gate cold. Clear the track completely.
  • Frozen rollers: In extreme cold, rollers can freeze. Apply a silicone-based lubricant (never WD-40, which attracts moisture and freezes).
  • Gate off track: If the gate has shifted or tilted, it may have jumped the track or derailed from the cantilever rollers. Do not force it — call for service.

Swing Gates

  • Snow in the swing path: If there's snow or ice where the gate needs to swing, it physically can't open. Clear the arc.
  • Hinge binding: Frost heave can shift gate posts, causing the gate to drag or bind on the hinges. Check that posts are plumb (vertical).
  • Operator arm disconnected: High winds or impacts can knock a swing gate arm loose. Check that the arm is still attached to the gate bracket.

Step 5: Gate Won't Latch or Close Fully

If your gate won't latch properly — it swings or slides to the closed position but doesn't lock — check these:

  • Latch alignment: Frost heave, ground settling, or a leaning post can shift the latch and receiver out of alignment. Loosen the mounting bolts and realign.
  • Limit switch adjustment: The operator has limit switches that tell it where "open" and "closed" are. If these are misadjusted, the gate may stop short of fully closing. Consult your operator manual for the limit adjustment procedure.
  • Magnetic lock: Electric magnetic locks (mag locks) need power to engage. Check the wiring and power supply to the lock.
  • Sagging gate: Over time, heavy gates sag on the hinge side. The bottom corner drags on the ground, preventing full closure. This requires structural repair — adjusting the hinges or adding a support wheel.

How to Manually Open Your Automatic Gate

Every automatic gate has a manual release for emergencies and power outages. Knowing how to use it before you need it is important:

  1. Locate the release. On most operators, there's a key cylinder or lever on the operator housing. For underground operators, there may be a key access point at ground level.
  2. Disengage the motor. Turn the key or flip the lever to release the gate from the motor drive.
  3. Move the gate manually. Slide or swing the gate by hand. It should move freely once released.
  4. Re-engage after power returns. Return the release to the locked position and cycle the gate once with the remote to reset the operator.
Keep your manual release key accessible Don't lock it in the house on the other side of the gate. Keep a copy in your vehicle, with a neighbour, or in a lockbox near the gate. In a power outage at -30°C, you don't want to be climbing a frozen fence.

How to Make a Gate More Stable

If your gate wobbles, sags, or doesn't track straight, these are the most common fixes:

  • Reinforce or replace posts: Gate posts take more stress than fence posts. They should be set in concrete at least 42 inches deep (below Winnipeg's frost line). Wood posts may need to be replaced with steel.
  • Add a support wheel: For heavy swing gates, a roller wheel at the bottom corner carries the weight and prevents sagging.
  • Tighten hardware: Hinges, brackets, and roller assemblies loosen over time from vibration. Tighten all bolts and replace any worn bushings.
  • Check the V-track: For slide gates, a bent or shifted track causes the gate to wobble. The track may need to be re-leveled.

When to Call for Professional Gate Repair

Call a gate repair service if:

  • The operator makes grinding, clicking, or buzzing noises but the gate doesn't move
  • The gate moved off the track or out of the rollers
  • You smell burning or see smoke from the operator
  • The control board shows error codes you can't clear
  • A gate post has shifted or is leaning significantly
  • Any safety device appears damaged or isn't responding
  • The gate struck a vehicle or person (safety devices need immediate inspection)

Prevent Future Problems: Gate Maintenance

Most gate breakdowns are preventable with regular gate maintenance service. We recommend:

  • Spring service: After winter, check all hardware, lubricate rollers and hinges, test safety devices, inspect wiring for rodent damage, and realign any components shifted by frost heave
  • Fall prep: Before winter, apply cold-weather lubricant, check battery condition, clear tracks and paths, and verify the manual release works
  • Monthly: Test the photo eyes and safety reverse, clean sensor lenses, and visually inspect for damage or loose hardware

A maintenance plan costs a fraction of an emergency gate repair call and keeps your gate running reliably through Manitoba's toughest weather.
