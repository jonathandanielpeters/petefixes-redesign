Hiring a Fence Contractor in Winnipeg: What to Expect from Quote to Completion

April 5, 2026 · 10 min read

Whether it's your first fence or a replacement for one that's seen too many Manitoba winters, hiring a fence contractor can feel overwhelming. There are dozens of companies in the Winnipeg market, quotes that vary wildly, and a long list of decisions to make about materials, styles, and timelines. This guide walks you through the entire process — from your first phone call to the final walkthrough — so you know exactly what to expect and how to get the best result for your money.

Step 1: Research and Shortlist Your Contractors

Before you pick up the phone, spend some time narrowing the field. Not all fence companies are the same, and the terminology can be confusing. You'll see companies describe themselves as fence installers, fence builders, fence carpenters, and fence contractors — and while there's overlap, these terms can signal different levels of service.

A fence installer typically focuses on assembling prefabricated fence panels or standard configurations. A fence builder or carpenter usually does more custom work — milling boards, building gates from scratch, handling unusual layouts. A fence contractor is the broadest term and usually means a company that handles the full scope: design, materials, installation, gates, and cleanup. For most residential projects, you want a contractor who does it all.

What to Look For

  • WCB coverage: Manitoba doesn't require a specific fence license, but any legitimate contractor should carry Workers Compensation Board (WCB) coverage. This protects you if a worker is injured on your property. Ask for their WCB clearance letter.
  • Liability insurance: A minimum of $2 million in commercial general liability insurance is standard. This covers damage to your property during installation — a broken sprinkler line, a scratched vehicle, a damaged garden bed.
  • Portfolio of completed work: Ask to see photos of recent projects, ideally in Winnipeg. Look for clean post lines, consistent board spacing, level top rails, and well-hung gates.
  • Reviews and references: Google reviews are a starting point, but ask for two or three recent references you can call. Ask those references about communication, timeline accuracy, and how the contractor handled any issues that came up.
  • Clear communication: How quickly do they return calls? Do they answer your questions directly? A contractor who's hard to reach before the job starts won't get easier to reach during or after it.

Red Flags

  • No written quote: A verbal estimate is not a quote. If a contractor won't put the price and scope in writing, walk away.
  • Large upfront deposit: A deposit of 10-30% is normal to secure materials. Anything over 50% upfront is a red flag.
  • No insurance documentation: If they can't produce WCB and liability certificates, they're either uninsured or don't want you to know.
  • Pressure to sign immediately: A good contractor is confident enough in their price and work that they'll give you time to compare quotes.

Step 2: The Quote Process

Once you've shortlisted two or three contractors, it's time to get quotes. Here's what the process typically looks like.

The Site Visit

A serious contractor will want to see your property before quoting. Phone or email quotes based on a rough description are unreliable — they miss slope changes, access issues, underground utilities, tree roots, and the condition of your existing fence. During the site visit, the contractor will:

  • Measure the fence line and mark the layout
  • Identify gate locations and sizes
  • Assess terrain — slopes, drainage, soil type
  • Check access for equipment and material delivery
  • Note any existing fence that needs removal
  • Discuss your preferences for style, height, and materials

What a Good Quote Includes

The quote you receive should be detailed enough that you could hand it to another contractor and they'd know exactly what's being proposed. At minimum, it should list:

  • Total linear footage of fence
  • Fence height and style (6-foot privacy, 4-foot chain link, etc.)
  • Material specifications: board type and size (1x6 pressure-treated, 2x6 cedar, etc.), post type (wood 4x4, metal driven, Postmaster), number of rails
  • Gates: number, type (pedestrian, double drive), and hardware
  • Old fence removal and disposal, if applicable
  • Total price with a breakdown of materials and labour
  • Estimated start date and completion timeline
  • Warranty terms

Step 3: Design and Materials

Once you've chosen a contractor and agreed on the scope, it's time to finalize the design. This is where your fence goes from "a fence" to your fence — and the decisions you make here affect the look, lifespan, and cost of the finished product.

Choosing Your Fence Type

Winnipeg contractors typically offer three main categories of fencing, each with variations. For a detailed breakdown of pricing by type, see our Winnipeg fence cost guide.

  • Wood fences: Pressure-treated and cedar are the two main options. Pressure-treated is more affordable and handles moisture well. Cedar is naturally rot-resistant, looks beautiful, and costs more. Styles range from standard vertical board to horizontal plank, board-on-board, shadowbox, and lattice-top designs. Custom fence builders can create almost any pattern you can draw.
  • Chain link fences: The workhorse of Winnipeg fencing. Available in galvanized (silver) or vinyl-coated (black, green, brown). Privacy slats can be woven in for screening. Chain link is the most affordable option and requires almost no maintenance.
  • Ornamental and iron fences: Aluminum and steel panel fences for front yards, pool enclosures, and decorative applications. Higher cost, but virtually maintenance-free and long-lasting.

Post Options Matter More Than You Think

The posts are the foundation of your fence, and in Winnipeg's freeze-thaw climate, the type of post you choose has a direct impact on how long your fence stays straight. Wood 4x4 posts are the traditional choice, but they rot at the soil line within 8 to 12 years in Manitoba's clay soil. Metal posts — whether Postmaster (hidden inside the fence) or powder-coated 2x2 steel (visible as a design element) — resist frost heave and last 30 or more years. For a deep dive on post types and why they matter in Winnipeg, read our metal fence post guide.

Commercial Fence Options

If you're fencing a commercial property — a storage yard, parking lot, warehouse, or multi-family complex — the requirements are different from residential work. Commercial fencing typically means taller chain link (8 feet or more), heavier gauge mesh, industrial-grade posts, and sometimes barbed wire or razor ribbon topping. You may also need access gates for vehicles, pedestrian turnstiles, or integration with access control systems. Make sure your contractor has experience with commercial-scale projects, not just residential backyards.

Step 4: Timeline and Scheduling

Winnipeg has a compressed fence season. The ground is frozen from roughly November through April, which means most fence installation happens between May and October. That six-month window creates a predictable cycle of demand.

Typical Lead Times

  • March–April: Quoting season. Contractors are booking their spring and summer schedules. This is the best time to get quotes and secure your spot — wait until June and you may be looking at a late-summer or fall installation.
  • May–June: The rush begins. Lead times are typically 3-4 weeks.
  • July–August: Peak season. Lead times stretch to 4-6 weeks or longer. Material availability can also become an issue during this period.
  • September–October: The season winds down. Lead times shorten, and some contractors offer end-of-season pricing to keep crews busy before freeze-up.

How Long Does Installation Take?

For a standard residential fence (100-200 linear feet), installation typically takes 1 to 3 days. Here's a rough breakdown:

  • Small job (under 100 feet, no old fence to remove): 1 day
  • Average residential (100-200 feet with a gate or two): 1-2 days
  • Large or complex job (200+ feet, multiple gates, old fence removal, slopes): 2-3 days

Weather delays are a reality in Manitoba. Rain turns clay soil into a slippery mess that's unsafe to work in and difficult to navigate with equipment. A good contractor will communicate proactively about weather delays and reschedule promptly.

Step 5: Installation Day

You've signed the quote, picked your materials, and the crew is booked. Here's what happens when they show up.

Site Preparation

If you have an old fence, the crew will start by removing it. This includes pulling posts, taking down boards or mesh, and hauling everything away. If the old posts were set in concrete, those footings need to come out too — which can be a significant amount of work depending on how deep and wide they were poured. Confirm with your contractor upfront whether old fence removal and disposal is included in the quote.

Before any posts go in, the crew will mark the fence line with string or chalk and confirm the layout against the agreed plan. This is when they verify gate locations, check for Manitoba Hydro or utility locates (always call before you dig), and make any final adjustments for slope or obstacles.

Post Installation

Posts go in first. With driven metal posts, a hydraulic post driver sets each post in about 3 to 5 minutes with no digging and no concrete. With traditional wood posts, each hole is augered or hand-dug, the post is set, plumbed, and braced, and concrete is poured and left to cure. The driven method is dramatically faster, cleaner, and produces less disruption to your yard.

Rails and Boards

Once the posts are set and aligned, horizontal rails are attached — typically 2x4 or 2x3 lumber for wood fences, or tension wire for chain link. Then the fence face goes on: vertical or horizontal boards for a wood fence, mesh for chain link, or panels for ornamental fencing. A skilled crew works methodically down the fence line, keeping boards level and spacing consistent.

Gates

Gate installation is one of the most important parts of the job. A poorly hung gate will sag, drag, or fail to latch within the first year. Gate posts need to be set deeper and stronger than line posts. The gate frame needs to be square, the hinges need to be heavy-duty, and the latch needs to engage cleanly. Double drive gates for vehicle access are more complex and require careful alignment so both panels meet evenly in the centre.

Crew Size and Daily Progress

Most residential fence crews are 2 to 4 people. A two-person crew with a post driver can typically set 25 to 40 posts in a day and build 80 to 120 linear feet of fence. Larger crews move faster but aren't always necessary for average residential jobs.

Post-Only Installation

If you're handy and want to save on labour, some contractors offer post-only installation. They set all the posts to the correct depth, spacing, and alignment, and you handle the rails, boards, or mesh yourself. This is especially popular with driven metal posts, since the hydraulic equipment is something most homeowners don't have access to. Expect to pay roughly 40 to 50 percent of a full installation for post-only work.

Step 6: Walkthrough and Warranty

When the crew finishes, walk the entire fence line with the contractor before signing off. Here's what to check:

  • Post alignment: Sight down the fence line from one end. Posts should be in a straight line (or a smooth curve on curves). No post should lean visibly in or out.
  • Board spacing: Boards should be evenly spaced with no large gaps (unless the design calls for them). Board tops should follow a consistent line — either flat across or stepping evenly with the terrain.
  • Gates: Open and close every gate. It should swing freely without dragging on the ground, latch securely, and not spring open on its own. Double gates should meet evenly.
  • Cleanup: The crew should remove all old fence materials, concrete debris, packaging, and hardware from your yard. The fence line should look finished, not like a construction zone.
  • Top rail and caps: If your fence has a top cap rail or post caps, check that they're secure and level.

What a Good Warranty Covers

Most reputable Winnipeg fence contractors offer a warranty on both workmanship and materials. A typical residential warranty covers:

  • Workmanship: 1 to 5 years. This covers installation defects — posts that lean, boards that pop off, gates that fail to latch properly. If something goes wrong because of how the fence was built, the contractor fixes it at no charge.
  • Materials: Varies by manufacturer. Metal posts often carry a 25-year or lifetime warranty against rust-through. Lumber warranties depend on the supplier and treatment type. Your contractor should pass through any manufacturer warranties on posts, hardware, and gate operators.

What a warranty typically does not cover: damage from storms, falling trees, vehicle impacts, or normal weathering of wood (greying, minor checking). Maintenance is your responsibility — see below.

Maintenance Responsibilities

Once the fence is yours, keeping it in good shape is on you. For wood fences, that means staining or sealing within the first year and every 2 to 3 years after that. Chain link and metal fences need almost no maintenance beyond occasional cleaning. Keeping vegetation trimmed away from the fence line prevents moisture damage and makes it easier to inspect for issues early.

Where to Buy Fence Parts Yourself

Sometimes you don't need a full fence build — you need a replacement rail, a bag of fence clips, or a new gate latch. Winnipeg has several options for sourcing fence parts and hardware.

Local building supply stores carry pressure-treated lumber, fence boards, and basic hardware. For specialty items like gate rollers, track for sliding gates, chain link fittings, or post brackets, you'll typically need to visit a dedicated fence supplier. These suppliers stock the parts that big box stores don't — the specific rail clips for Postmaster posts, heavy-duty gate hinges rated for daily use, and the mesh and tension bands for commercial chain link.

If you're looking for gate rollers and track for a sliding or cantilever gate, these are specialty items that most general hardware stores don't carry. A fence supply shop or your fence contractor can source the correct roller type, track gauge, and mounting hardware for your specific gate. Financing a Fence Project

A new fence is a significant investment — a typical Winnipeg residential fence runs between $4,000 and $8,000 depending on size and materials. That's a meaningful number for most households, and it's worth asking about payment options.

Some fence contractors offer payment plans or work with third-party financing providers that let you spread the cost over 6 to 24 months. Others accept credit card payments, which gives you access to your card's built-in payment flexibility. A few options to explore:

  • Contractor financing: Ask your contractor directly if they offer payment plans. Some do, some don't — it depends on the company.
  • Home improvement loans: Your bank or credit union may offer a personal line of credit or home improvement loan at a lower interest rate than a credit card.
  • Property tax financing: Some municipalities allow homeowners to finance property improvements through their property tax bill, though this is less common for fences than for major renovations.

Whatever route you choose, get the financing sorted before you sign the contract. Knowing your budget upfront helps you make material decisions without guessing.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to get a fence installed in Winnipeg?

From first contact to finished fence, expect 2 to 6 weeks depending on the season. During peak months (June through August), lead times stretch to 4-6 weeks. In spring and fall, you can often get on the schedule within 2-3 weeks. The actual installation typically takes 1-3 days for a standard residential fence.

Do I need a permit to build a fence in Winnipeg?

For standard residential fences up to 6 feet tall, Winnipeg does not require a building permit. However, you must follow the City of Winnipeg's zoning bylaws for setbacks and height limits. Your fence contractor should know these requirements and factor them into the design. Corner lots and fences taller than 6 feet may have additional restrictions.

What should a fence quote include?

A thorough fence quote should include total linear footage, fence height and style, material specifications (board type, post type, rail count), number and type of gates, removal and disposal of the old fence if applicable, a clear total price, estimated start date, and warranty terms. Be wary of quotes that list only a lump sum with no material breakdown.

Can I hire a contractor to install just the posts and do the boards myself?

Yes. Post-only installation is a common option for handy homeowners who want the structural work done professionally but plan to attach the boards, rails, or mesh themselves. This is especially popular with driven metal posts, which require hydraulic equipment most homeowners don't have. Expect to pay roughly 40-50% of a full fence installation for post-only work.
