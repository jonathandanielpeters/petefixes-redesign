Fence Repair in Winnipeg: Chain Link, Wood Posts & Storm Damage Guide

April 5, 2026 · 9 min read

Winnipeg's weather takes a toll on fences. Between high winds, frost heave, heavy snow loads, and the occasional vehicle backing into a fence panel, damage is inevitable. This guide covers how to diagnose and repair the most common fence problems — and when it's worth calling a professional instead of fixing it yourself.

How to Repair Chain Link Fences

Chain link is durable, but it can be dented by impacts, sag from failed tension, or develop rust holes over time. Here are the most common chain link fence repairs:

Fixing a Bent or Dented Section

If a section of chain link has been pushed in (from a vehicle, fallen tree, or heavy snow), you can often reshape it:

  1. Disconnect the damaged section by removing the tension bar and bands at the nearest post
  2. Lay the mesh flat on the ground and straighten it by hand or with pliers
  3. Re-attach the mesh to the posts using new tension bands and a tension bar
  4. Re-tension with a fence stretcher (or come-along) until the mesh is taut

Patching a Hole

For small holes or cuts in chain link:

  1. Cut a patch piece of matching chain link mesh, slightly larger than the hole
  2. Unweave the top and bottom wires of the patch
  3. Position the patch over the hole and weave the loose wires through the existing mesh
  4. Bend the wire ends over to secure the patch
  5. For a cleaner look, use hog rings or fence ties to attach the patch edges

Replacing a Leaning Post

Chain link posts lean when the concrete footing cracks or the post rusts at ground level. To replace:

  1. Disconnect the mesh from the leaning post
  2. Dig out the old post and concrete footing
  3. Set the new post in fresh concrete, at least 36 inches deep (42 inches is better for Winnipeg's frost line)
  4. Let concrete cure for 24-48 hours before re-attaching the mesh

How to Repair Wood Posts

Rotting posts are the number one wood fence repair in Winnipeg. Posts rot at ground level where moisture, soil, and air meet — the perfect conditions for decay.

Option 1: Post Mender (Quick Fix)

A steel post mender is a bracket that bolts onto the good wood above the rot and has a spike or base that anchors into the ground. It's faster and cheaper than full replacement. Best for posts that are rotted at the base but solid above 12 inches.

Option 2: Sister Post

Bolt a new shorter post alongside the existing post, from above the rot line down into fresh concrete. The original post stays in place and the sister post provides structural support. Good for posts where you don't want to disassemble the fence sections.

Option 3: Full Replacement

Dig out the old post, set a new one in concrete. If you're replacing multiple wood posts, this is a good time to switch to metal posts (Postmaster or 2x2 steel) that won't rot. Metal post replacement eliminates this repair forever.

Prevention Tip When installing new wood fence posts in Winnipeg, wrap the below-ground portion with roofing tar or use posts rated for ground contact (UC4A treated lumber). Setting posts in concrete — not just tamped soil — dramatically extends their life in our clay soils.

How to Repair a Broken Gate

Gates take more abuse than any other part of a fence. They swing, get pushed, sag under their own weight, and are the first thing to fail. Common gate repairs:

Sagging Gate

A gate that drags on the ground or won't latch because it's dropped on the latch side:

  • Install an anti-sag kit: A diagonal cable with a turnbuckle that runs from the bottom hinge corner to the top latch corner. Tighten the turnbuckle to lift the sagging corner.
  • Replace hinges: Worn hinges allow the gate to drop. Heavy-duty, adjustable hinges let you fine-tune the gate position.
  • Add a wheel: A support roller on the bottom corner of the gate carries the weight and prevents future sagging. Essential for wide or heavy gates.

Gate Won't Latch

Misalignment is almost always the cause. Frost heave shifts the posts, and the latch and striker no longer line up. Fixes:

  • Loosen the latch or striker plate mounting bolts and realign
  • If the post has shifted, re-plumb the post (may require digging and re-setting)
  • Replace a worn latch mechanism — they're inexpensive and available at any fence supply store

How to Make a Gate More Stable

Gate posts need to be stronger than line posts because they carry the swinging weight of the gate. To stabilize a wobbly gate:

  • Beef up the posts: Gate posts should be 4x6 or 6x6 (not 4x4) for wood, or heavy-gauge steel for metal. Set them in at least 3 bags of concrete each.
  • Add a header: A horizontal beam connecting the two gate posts at the top prevents them from spreading apart under the gate's weight.
  • Use three hinges: Three hinges distribute the gate weight better than two, reducing stress on each hinge point.

How to Build a Gate

Building a fence gate yourself? Here's the simplified approach:

  1. Measure the opening. Leave 1/2 inch clearance on each side for hinge and latch hardware.
  2. Build the frame. Use 2x4 lumber for the top, bottom, and sides. Add a diagonal brace from the bottom hinge corner to the top latch corner — this prevents sagging.
  3. Attach pickets. Use the same boards as your fence. Space them to match the fence pattern.
  4. Install hinges. Use heavy-duty, rust-proof hinges rated for the gate weight. Position one 6 inches from the top and one 6 inches from the bottom.
  5. Add a latch. Self-closing latches are best for dog owners. Gravity latches or spring latches are simplest.

Where to Buy Fence Parts in Winnipeg

If you're doing fence repairs yourself, you'll need parts. Here's where to find fence parts and hardware locally:

  • Fence supply dealers: Dedicated fence suppliers carry professional-grade parts — chain link fittings, tension bars, post caps, rails, gate hardware, and specialty items like gate rollers and track for slide gates. They can also cut chain link mesh to your exact length.
  • Hardware stores: Home Depot, Rona, and Home Hardware carry basic fence parts — pickets, rails, post brackets, hinges, latches, and concrete. Selection varies by location.
  • Online suppliers: For specialty parts like cantilever gate rollers, specific operator brackets, or commercial-grade hardware, online fence supply stores often have the best selection.

Storm Damage: What to Do After High Winds

Winnipeg gets severe thunderstorms and occasional tornado-strength winds. If your fence is damaged in a storm:

  1. Document the damage. Take photos and video before touching anything. You'll need this for insurance claims.
  2. Check for hazards. Downed fence sections near power lines, exposed nails, or broken glass are safety risks. Keep pets and children away.
  3. Secure loose sections. If fence panels are hanging or leaning, tie them to intact posts with rope or wire to prevent further damage or injury.
  4. Contact your insurance. Most homeowners' policies cover fence damage from storms, wind, and fallen trees. File the claim promptly.
  5. Get repair quotes. Get at least two quotes from professional fence installers or repair services. Insurance will want these.

How to Keep People from Breaking Into Your Fence

If security is a concern — vandalism, break-ins, or theft — consider these fence upgrades:

  • Anti-climb fencing: Chain link with small mesh (1-inch) or welded mesh panels are harder to climb than standard chain link
  • Cut-proof fencing: Heavy-gauge welded mesh or steel bar fencing resists bolt cutters. Used for commercial fence and industrial applications
  • Security hardware: Tamper-proof screws, concealed hinges, and heavy-duty locks on gates
  • Fence-top deterrents: Angled extensions, razor ribbon (commercial only — not allowed on residential fences), or rotating anti-climb devices
  • Lighting and cameras: Motion-activated lights and security cameras are often more effective deterrents than the fence itself

Repair vs Replace: When to Stop Fixing

Sometimes repairing individual sections costs more than replacing the whole fence. Consider a full replacement when:

  • More than 30% of posts are rotted or leaning
  • The fence is over 15-20 years old and showing widespread deterioration
  • You're spending more on annual repairs than the amortized cost of a new fence
  • You want to change the fence type or style anyway
  • The fence no longer meets current Winnipeg fence bylaws
