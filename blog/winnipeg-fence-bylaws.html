Winnipeg Fence Bylaws 2026: Height Rules, Pool Fences & Everything You Need to Know

April 5, 2026 · 8 min read

Planning to build a fence in Winnipeg? Before you pick out materials or get quotes from a fence contractor, you need to understand the local bylaws. Getting the rules wrong can mean tearing down your brand-new fence — and nobody wants that.

This guide covers every bylaw and regulation that affects residential and commercial fence installation in Winnipeg, from height limits to pool fences to property line requirements. We keep it updated as rules change, so bookmark this page.

Fence Height Rules in Winnipeg

The most common question we get from homeowners is: how tall can my fence be in Winnipeg? The answer depends on where on your property the fence sits.

Location Maximum Height Notes
Rear yard (back lane side) 2.0 m (6'6") Most common for privacy fences
Side yard (interior lot line) 2.0 m (6'6") From the rear yard to the front building line
Front yard 1.2 m (4'0") Measured from the front building line forward
Corner lot (flanking street side) 1.2 m (4'0") in sight triangle Visibility for traffic safety — varies by intersection

Can I Make My Fence Taller Than 6 Feet?

The bylaw caps residential fences at 2.0 metres in rear and side yards. If you need a taller fence for a commercial property, industrial site, or cellular tower fencing, you may need to apply for a variance through the City of Winnipeg's planning department. Specialty applications like cut-proof fencing for high-security sites often require heights above the standard limit.

For residential properties, there are creative ways to add privacy without exceeding the height limit — lattice toppers, strategic landscaping, or privacy fence inserts that attach to your existing fence.

Do I Need a Permit to Build a Fence in Winnipeg?

Good news: standard residential fences in Winnipeg do not require a building permit. You can build a wood fence, chain link fence, or ornamental fence without going through the permit process, as long as you follow the height restrictions and property line rules.

However, there are exceptions:

  • Retaining walls combined with fences may require a permit if the total height exceeds limits
  • Pool enclosures must meet specific safety requirements (see below)
  • Commercial fencing in certain zones may have additional zoning requirements

Pool Fence Bylaws in Manitoba

If you own a pool, hot tub, or any water feature holding more than 24 inches (610 mm) of water, Manitoba law requires a barrier fence. This applies to both in-ground and above-ground pools.

Pool Fence Requirements Minimum height: 1.5 metres (5 feet). The fence must be non-scalable — no horizontal rails, chain link with small mesh only, or solid boards. Gates must be self-closing and self-latching, with the latch on the pool side at least 1.5 m from the ground.

Key rules for pool fences in Winnipeg:

  • The fence must completely enclose the pool area — no gaps a child could fit through
  • Openings in the fence (mesh, pickets) cannot exceed 100 mm (4 inches)
  • The bottom of the fence must be no more than 100 mm above ground level
  • Your house wall can count as one side of the enclosure, but any doors opening to the pool area need self-closing hardware
  • Above-ground pools with walls at least 1.5 m tall may satisfy the barrier requirement, but stairs and ladders must be removable or gated

Failing to fence your pool properly isn't just a bylaw violation — it's a serious liability issue. If you're unsure whether your current fence meets pool fence bylaws in Manitoba, we offer free assessments.

Where Is My Property Line?

One of the most common fence disputes in Winnipeg comes down to property lines. Build your fence even a few inches onto your neighbour's property, and you could be forced to tear it down.

How Far Is the Property Line from the Curb in Winnipeg?

There's no single answer. In Winnipeg, the distance from the curb (or sidewalk, or back lane) to your actual property line varies by neighbourhood, lot configuration, and the age of the subdivision. Common setbacks include:

  • Front property line to sidewalk: Typically 1.0 to 3.0 metres of city boulevard, but it varies
  • Rear property line to back lane: Usually right at the edge of the lane, but not always
  • Side property line: Defined by your lot survey — there's no standard offset from sidewalks or streets

The only reliable way to know where your property ends is a survey or staking certificate from a Manitoba Land Surveyor.

Do I Need a Survey for a Fence Installation?

Legally, you don't need a survey to build a fence in Winnipeg. But we strongly recommend one. Here's why:

  • A staking certificate from the Association of Manitoba Land Surveyors typically costs $500 to $800 — far less than tearing down and rebuilding a misplaced fence
  • Your neighbour can request removal of any fence that encroaches on their property
  • If you're building a fence on or near the property line, knowing the exact boundary prevents disputes
  • Some professional fence installers in Winnipeg won't start work without a survey to protect both parties

Fence Between Neighbours: Who Pays?

Manitoba's Boundary Lines and Line Fences Act covers shared fences. If your fence sits directly on the property line, both neighbours share the cost of building and maintaining it — in theory.

In practice, here's how it usually works:

  • If both neighbours agree to the fence, they typically split the cost 50/50
  • If only one neighbour wants the fence, they usually pay for it and build it on their own property (set back from the line)
  • If there's a dispute, either party can apply to a municipal board for a resolution
  • The "good side" (finished face) traditionally faces outward toward the neighbour, though this is custom, not law
Pro Tip Before building, talk to your neighbours. A quick conversation about fence style, height, and cost-sharing prevents 90% of disputes. If you're replacing an existing shared fence, give your neighbour reasonable written notice.

What Are the Rules for Fence Height in Manitoba Outside Winnipeg?

If you live in a municipality outside Winnipeg (like the RM of Springfield, East St. Paul, West St. Paul, or Headingley), fence regulations are set by your local municipal government. Rules vary, but most follow similar guidelines to Winnipeg's 2.0 m rear/side and 1.2 m front limits. Always check with your local planning office.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I attach anything to my neighbour's fence?

No. Your neighbour's fence is their property. You cannot attach planters, privacy screens, or anything else to a fence you don't own without their permission.

Can I build a fence right on the property line?

Yes, but both neighbours then share ownership and maintenance responsibility under Manitoba law. Most homeowners set the fence 1 to 2 inches inside their own property line to avoid shared-ownership complications.

Does my HOA or condo board have fence rules?

Possibly. Homeowner associations and condo corporations in Winnipeg can impose additional restrictions on fence style, colour, and materials. Check your declaration and bylaws before building.

What happens if my fence doesn't meet the bylaw?

The City of Winnipeg can issue a compliance order requiring you to modify or remove the fence. This usually happens after a neighbour complaint. Fines may apply for non-compliance.
